    What it does

    Four screens do most of the work. Here's each one, and what it's for.

    Schedule

    3 pm and 8 pm stop feeling the same

    Your day drawn to scale, not stacked in a list. You can see the gaps, the crunch, and what's next — with a reminder at the start and end of every block.

    Check-in

    How it went, in two taps

    Did it happen, and how do you feel? One tap for each. Moods are faces with names — Rough to Great — not a number to optimize. Five seconds, then back to your day.

    Dashboard

    Your whole day, at a glance

    A goal to steer by, then one tile per tracker — blocks done, mood, meals, spend. Tap a span to zoom out to a week, a month, a quarter, a year, and the trends arrive as bars.

    You log a face, never a score. Zoomed out it becomes a word and a number — Good · 3.7 of 5 — so a trend stays readable without the moment ever being graded.

    Light or dark — your call. It follows your phone.

    People

    The people you keep meaning to text

    See who's coming up, and who it's been a while with. A hello earns a small celebration, and a quick note after keeps the thread — so the next one starts somewhere.

    What else you can track

    Schedule and check-ins are the core, and they're always on. These are optional — each one off until you turn it on, so day one isn't a setup screen.

    Meals

    Pin what you eat often. Log it again in a tap.

    Exercise

    A walk counts. Log movement in a tap and watch the week add up.

    Sleep

    Log a night by hand, or read it from Apple Health. It only reads sleep, and only if you connect it.

    Budget

    Amount and category. A record, not a budgeting system.

    Brain dump

    A blank page for whatever's loud in your head. The one place where typing is the point.

    History

    Everything you've logged, grouped by day. Filter to one tracker, tap an entry to edit it, or reopen a check-in to add to what you wrote.

    Patterns

    After a few weeks of logging, patterns you can check surface quietly — like steadier moods on days you moved. It shows how sure it is, and won't claim which came first unless the timing says so.

    Why it's like this

    Every ADHD app promises to be different. Here are the actual decisions, with what they replaced.

    No streaks, no pet, no guilt

    A broken streak becomes a reason to quit. Coming back should feel like nothing happened.

    Instead of: points, virtual pets, "don't break the chain."

    Undo, not "are you sure?"

    Confirmation dialogs get dismissed on reflex. Every delete is instant, with five seconds to take it back.

    Instead of: dialogs nobody reads.

    Never lock between impulse and log

    Face ID protects your data. But a check-in from a notification opens above the lock, because the moment doesn't wait.

    Instead of: security that costs the log.

    Skipped isn't red

    A block you didn't do turns gray, not red. Tap it any time to reflect — the mood you log there keeps its full color even though the block doesn't.

    Instead of: overdue badges.

    People are part of focus

    Losing touch is an ADHD casualty too. Reaching out gets a small celebration, and you can jot what you talked about so the next one has somewhere to start.

    Instead of: "overdue: call Mom (12 days)."

    Five more decisions

    Two taps, zero typing

    Every extra tap is a drop-off point. Chips are templates you pin, so your one-tap row holds what you actually repeat.

    Instead of: auto-filled recents. A $340 flight is recent; it isn't re-loggable.

    A timeline, not a list

    Blocks drawn to scale make the shape of the day visible. That's the direct counter to time blindness.

    Instead of: the checklist.

    The widget invites, it doesn't nag

    It knows the time of day, which trackers you use, and what the block in front of it actually is. After 9 pm it goes quiet.

    Instead of: "Drink some water!" and taps that lead nowhere.

    Type or talk

    A mic sits next to the longer fields — a note, the brain dump, a reach-out. Recognition runs on your device wherever the system allows it.

    Instead of: sending your voice off-device to save you some typing.

    A direction to come back to

    Set one thing you're working toward and the next small step. It sits on your Today screen and inside the block you're in, so the goal is there at the moment you'd usually drift from it.

    Instead of: a goal system that measures you and becomes one more thing to fall behind on.

    Questions

    Is this a medical or therapy app?

    No. It's a personal secretary: schedule, logs, reminders. No diagnoses, no advice, no clinical claims.

    Do I have to set everything up first?

    No. Schedule and check-ins work from the first minute. Turn on meals, exercise, sleep, budget, or the brain dump whenever you want them.

    Where does my data live?

    On your device. iCloud sync is an optional toggle, off by default, and goes through your own iCloud account.

    Does it listen to me?

    Only when you tap the mic. It's there for fields worth talking instead of typing, and recognition runs on your device wherever the system supports it.

    Does it use Apple Health?

    Only if you connect it, and only to read your sleep. Nothing is written back. You can log sleep by hand instead, or leave it off.

    Will it tell me what's affecting my mood?

    After a few weeks of real data, it surfaces gentle leads — like better moods on days you exercised. It shows a confidence level, and won't claim which came first unless the timing supports it. Leads to notice, not diagnoses.

    Android? Web?

    iPhone first. The Android build is real and in closed testing — a small group is running it while the last pieces land, and it opens up when it's genuinely ready rather than when it's merely installable. No web app — it's built to live on your phone.

    When can I try it?

    It's open. Join the beta on TestFlight. Free, private, and there's no AideyHD account to make.

    It takes about two minutes: install Apple's TestFlight app, tap Join, done. Apple runs the beta, which is why there's no signup form here — and why you'll need an Apple ID, the one you already use for the App Store. I never see it.

    Once you're in, there's a Send feedback button in Settings that comes straight to me — tell me what's working and what isn't. TestFlight has its own way too: screenshot the app, then tap Share Beta Feedback.

    What happens to my logs when the beta ends?

    They stay. Your entries live in the app's own storage on your phone, not on a server of mine that could go away — beta builds update in place and your data carries across them, and on into the public version when it ships.

    Apple retires each beta build 90 days after it's posted, so there'll be a newer one well before then. And if you'd rather hold your own copy regardless, Settings ▸ Export hands you every entry as CSV or JSON, any time. That's the real answer: it's your data, and you can walk out with it whenever you like.
