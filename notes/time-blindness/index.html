Time blindness, and why a list won't fix it

ADHD & design notes · about 4 minutes · draft

The short version. Write 2–3 sentences that give away the whole argument up front (the ADHD reader who bounces still leaves with the point). Rough shape: time blindness makes the gap between now and later hard to feel; a to-do list hides that gap entirely; a day drawn to scale puts it back. Say it in your own words.

A day that got away from me

Your story, one concrete scene. Open with a specific moment your own time blindness cost you something, told like you'd tell a friend. Not the definition, the felt thing.

  • What happened? (The deadline that arrived "out of nowhere," the afternoon that evaporated, being late even though you left "on time.")
  • What did it feel like in the moment, and just after?
  • Before AideyHD, what had you tried, and how did each thing fall short for you specifically?

What time blindness actually is

The idea, plainly and honestly. A short, research-grounded explanation, hedged (write "research suggests," never "ADHD causes"). Keep it to a paragraph or two.

  • In your own felt words, what is time blindness? (e.g. "3 pm and 8 pm feel the same distance away.")
  • Is there research, a book, or a person who shaped how you understand ADHD and time that you'd want to point readers to? (Russell Barkley on executive function and time is the usual anchor.)
  • Anything you want me to research and draft for this section with real citations? I can hand you a sourced paragraph to edit.

Why a list makes it worse

The core argument. This is the load-bearing section, and it's the one only you can make from the inside.

  • What does a to-do list hide that time doesn't forgive? (A list has no duration and no distance. Every item looks the same size.)
  • Why does that specifically trip an ADHD brain, in your experience?
  • What was the exact frustration or insight that made you stop using a list?

What helps: a day you can see

The design response. Now connect it to what AideyHD does, without turning it into a sales pitch. Show the reasoning, not the feature list.

  • Why draw the day to scale instead of as a list? What did that change for you?
  • Which other Schedule choices are aimed at time blindness (the now-line, reminders at the start and end of a block, the visible empty gaps), and which matters most?
  • What did you deliberately not do, and why? (No countdown anxiety, no red overdue.)

What this isn't

A calendar drawn to scale is a scaffold, not a cure. It won't fix time blindness, and it isn't treatment for anything. AideyHD isn't a medical or therapy app, and nothing here is medical advice. It's one tool that made my own days easier to see, shared in case it does the same for yours.

Optional: keep the paragraph above, or say this boundary in your own voice. The one rule: don't imply the app treats ADHD.
